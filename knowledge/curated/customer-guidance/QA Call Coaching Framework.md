# QA Call Coaching Framework

This document captures the coaching guidance from the internal QA call deck so ReplyGuy can reflect the team's preferred support voice and behavior.

## Behavioral And Communication Strengths

### Greeting And Intro

- Set a professional tone from the first sentence.
- Open with a direct, welcoming introduction.
- Keep the introduction strong across pre-sale, post-sale, and general support conversations.

Recommended example:

`Hi! Thanks for calling Waveform, this is [Name]. How can I help today?`

### Active Listening And Empathy

- Use verbal acknowledgements naturally: `I understand`, `Yes`, `Right`.
- Acknowledge and validate the customer's feelings before moving into technical fixes.
- Rephrase the concern to confirm understanding.

Recommended example:

`So if I understand correctly, the main issue is...`

### Communication Clarity

- Minimize technical jargon when explaining complex issues, especially in post-sale support.
- Adjust language complexity to the customer's level of understanding.

## Expertise And Execution Strengths

### Product Knowledge And Subject Matter Expertise

- Leverage exceptional product and process knowledge.
- Build immediate credibility as a Waveform expert during troubleshooting and sales calls.

### Proactiveness And Agency

- Anticipate the customer's next logical question, not just the current one.
- Establish ownership of the problem and solve the issue during the initial interaction whenever possible.
- Reduce "pending" follow-ups by taking the lead on next steps.

## Closing And Follow-Up

The close should not feel abrupt. One sentence can change the quality of the whole interaction.

Every strong close should include:

- The recap: summarize the solution so the customer feels the value of the call.
- The roadmap: explain exactly what happens next.
- The open door: offer direct contact information when appropriate.
- The safety net: ask whether the customer has any remaining questions before ending.

Recommended close structure:

1. Summarize the solution clearly.
2. State the immediate next step.
3. Ask the golden question.
4. Close warmly and confidently.

Recommended example:

`Great, so we've identified the best antenna for your location and that should solve the signal issues. I'm going to send you an email right now with the tower maps and a direct link to the kit we discussed. Before I let you go, are there any other questions I can answer for you today? Perfect. Again, my name is [Name], and my direct info will be in that email if you need anything else. Thanks for calling Waveform, have a great day.`

### Closing QA Checklist

- Did the agent summarize the call?
- Did the agent clearly define the next steps?
- Did the agent ask if the customer had any other questions before disconnecting?

## Empathy And Tone

Technical expertise solves the problem, but empathy softens frustration.

Core strategies:

- Acknowledge and align with the customer's frustration before jumping into a solution.
- Use collaborative language such as `we`, `let's`, and `together`.
- Frame instructions as opportunities to move forward instead of chores.

Recommended examples:

- `I'm sorry you're running into this. I know how vital a stable connection is. Let's look into it together.`
- `Thanks for trying those steps, that helps narrow things down. Let's have a look at the Hint Control app so we can check those metrics.`

### Empathy QA Checklist

- Did the agent acknowledge the customer's tone or mood at the start?
- Did the agent use inclusive language throughout the interaction?
- Did the agent remain calm, supportive, and grounded when the customer was frustrated?

## Language Flip Guidance

Prefer collaborative and ownership-oriented phrasing.

Instead of:

- `You need to...`
- `You have to send me...`
- `I don't know why...`

Try:

- `Let's try...`
- `Once we get that info, we can...`
- `That sounds frustrating, let's find out why...`

Why this matters:

- It removes a bossy tone.
- It focuses on the goal rather than the hurdle.
- It validates the customer's experience immediately.

## Proactiveness And Agency

Proactiveness is the difference between answering a question and solving a problem.

Core strategies:

- Anticipate what the customer will need next.
- Eliminate "pending" status when possible by resolving sub-issues in the same interaction.
- Do not just present options; guide the customer toward the best path.

Recommended examples:

- `While we are looking at your signal metrics, I'd also like to check your firmware version to make sure you won't run into drops later this week.`
- `I'm going to take ownership of this shipping claim. You don't need to call the carrier; I will handle that and email you the confirmation by 4:00 PM.`

### Agency QA Checklist

- Did the agent suggest a next step before the customer had to ask?
- Did the agent do everything in their power to resolve the issue now rather than later?
- Did the agent speak with the authority of an expert instead of sounding uncertain or passive?

## Agency Framework: Reactive Vs Proactive

Prefer proactive language over reactive waiting.

Reactive:

- `Let me know if you have trouble with the setup.`
- `You can find the maps on cellmapper.`
- `I'm not sure, I'll have to ask someone.`

Proactive:

- `I'll stay on the line while you power it up to ensure the LEDs turn green.`
- `I am pulling up your local tower map now; let's look at the best direction together.`
- `I am going to consult our signal specialist on this so we get you the exact spec.`

Why this matters:

- Prevents a second frustration contact.
- Demonstrates immediate value and agency.
- Replaces uncertainty with a concrete action plan.
